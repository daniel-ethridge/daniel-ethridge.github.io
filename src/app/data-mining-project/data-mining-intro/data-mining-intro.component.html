A Data Mining Approach for Advice on Surviving the Videogame Industry

The video game industry is a multi-billion dollar industry, and the evaluation continues to grow each year. Developments in technology have taken us from playing pong on the Atari system to virtual concerts inside of the wildly successful videogame Fortnite. Games such as the massive multiplayer online roleplaying games World of Warcraft and Guild Wars 2 allow players to play together and interact with each other in real-time when their geographical location might be thousands of miles apart. Additionally, continued development gave rise to Steam.com which is now the most popular distributor of PC/desktop games. While many of these games are developed by larger companies, the development of video game engines (which allow virtually anyone to make their own game granted they have necessary skills like software development) has led to a rise in independent video game development (or indie games).

The backgrounds of indie game developers varies widely. Some have worked in large professional studios, some have degrees ranging from computer science to game design, and some are gamers that are interested in making their own video games. Skill levels, knowledge levels, and experience vary widely. It follows that some indie developers know the process of building and shipping a full game with the potential of success while others are lost without a roadmap in an already cut-throat and competitive industry. While more and more game development resources are available on the internet in the forms of written instruction and YouTube.com video content, the backgrounds and expertise of the resource creators is often unknown. Viewers must take caution with instructional content due to potential incorrectness or a lack of best practices in the advice and knowledge presented.

In the case that the source of information is from a reputable or known source, the breadth of the information shared might be limited. Take the site Steamlytics.com and their entry for the widly popular indie game Hollow Knight. Steamlytics provides a sentiment analysis and a high level overview of the overarching response to the game. Information provided for the game (and others) includes general liking for the game, aggregated pros and cons, a positive and negative word clouds, bigrams, and trigrams. While the information could be helpful to know why Hollow Knight suceeded, it is difficult to learn any specifics. It would also be overly time consuming to analyze several games such that one could investigate maybe 10 to 12 in an hour. Doing that repeatedly would be a monotonous process. Similar websites like gamalytic.com and vginsights.com provide high level information similar to Steamlytics, but the helpfulness suffers from the same lack of depth. Additionally, some information is behind a paywall, increasing the barrier of entry.

These limitations of current instructional content for video game development could be at least in part supplemented by an in-depth data-driven analysis of factors that may predict the success or failure of a new video game. Such data can be collected from Steam. Each game on Steam has associated images, trailers, music, reviews, popularity scores, etc. This data can provide insight into potential reasons for the success or failure of a given video game. It is reasonable to expect that through analyzing thousands of games (on Steam, there are over 200,000), patterns will emerge in terms of the qualities that successful games share, that moderately successful games share, and that failed games share ,. These patterns could then be interpreted to inform guidelines and instructional content related to video game development. Futhermore, the patterns could be utilized to inform game marketing strategies which is a critical part of game success.

For indie game developers, the benefit is clear. Analyzing patterns in game reviews, sound tracks, reviews, and screenshots can provide insight into shared key attributes among successful video games. This includes actual game development and design choices to marketing strategies involving screenshots and trailers. Additionally, information such as this could be interesting and useful for large game companies like Valve (the owner of Steam.com) as well as players not involved in any game development process. For players, this information could lead to further developments in recommendation systems. Any reviews that a player leaves on games could be used in a sentiment analysis to better recommend other games that he or she may enjoy. On the same logic, better recommendation systems could obviously benefit a larger company like Valve by more efficiently connecting players with content that they enjoy but do not know exists.

Questions

  1. Do indie games tend to perform differently compared to AAA titles (ie from large professional studios) in terms of user feedback?
  2. To what extent can we predict a game's success (as measured by reviews and ratings) based on promotional materials (description, screenshots, trailers)?
  3. How to game trailers impact user reviews and ratings?
  4. Do certain genres generally perform better in terms of user ratings?
  5. How to regional preferences shape the success of specific game genres?
  6. To what degree are the length of game trailers related to a game's user ratings?
  7. How does social media activity influence the success of a game?
  8. How do game reviews change over time post-release?
  9. Are there specific genres of music commonly found in successful games?
  10. What compositional elements in soundtracks are correlated with positive user reviews?
